the baking formula and bakers percentage

the baking formula is a common language bakers use when they speak of bread recipes, or things like dough hydration levels or flour mix ratios.
the basic idea is:

  • disregard the weights, and express all quantities as percentage
  • the flours make up 100%
  • every other quantity is then calculated relative to those percents (this derived unit is known as bakers percentage)

for example, the ingredient list on a bread recipe might look like this:

  • 300 g bread flour
  • 100 g whole wheat flour
  • 260 mL water
  • 10 g active dry yeast
  • 1 tsp. fine salt
  • 3 tsp. sugar
  • 30 mL olive oil

to express this as a formula, convert everything to weight units, then follow the rules above.
after some rounding, this would give:

  • 75 bread flour
  • 25 whole wheat flour
  • 65 water
  • 2.5 active dry yeast
  • 2 fine salt
  • 4 sugar
  • 7 olive oil

this reveals some previously obsecured info, like the flour to water ratio (aka the hydration level), or the proportions in the flour mix.
also, now the recipe can be easily scaled up or down to any weight!

loaf weight

in this website, a single loaf will mean 400 g = 100 BP, in a standard size loaf pan.
some fatter, slow-rising, doughs (like brioche), require smaller portions for the same pan - around 250-300 g = 100 BP.

formulas and dough starters

sometimes the recipe will call for a dough starter (or "pre-ferment").
for sourdough starters, as the flour/water ratio is always known (1:1), the formula will simply include an additional component, e.g.:

  • 75 bread flour
  • 25 whole wheat flour
  • 15 starter
  • etc. …

for other starters (e.g. biga, poolish), there will be a second formula in place, for the starter ratios.
after adding both formulas, the flours will sum up to 100%.
in those recipes, expect something like this:

biga starter formula
  • 75 bread flour
  • 55 water
  • 0.25 active dry yeast
dough formula
  • 25 bread flour
  • 15 water
  • 1.5 active dry yeast
  • 2 salt
